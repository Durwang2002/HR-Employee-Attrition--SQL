1. Attrition is concentrated in specific departments

Departments with the highest attrition rate should be prioritized for employee retention initiatives, workload assessments, and manager reviews.

2. Certain job roles experience significantly higher attrition

3. Overtime is associated with higher attrition

Employees who frequently work overtime are more likely to leave the organization, suggesting the need for improved workload planning and work-life balance.

4. Lower job satisfaction is linked to employee turnover

Employees with lower Job Satisfaction scores are more likely to appear in the attrition group, highlighting employee engagement as a key retention factor.

5. Work-Life Balance impacts employee retention

Poor Work-Life Balance scores are more common among employees who leave, indicating that flexible work policies and well-being initiatives may improve retention.

6. Salary differences exist across departments and job roles

Average Monthly Income varies considerably between departments and job roles, making compensation benchmarking important for maintaining fairness and competitiveness.

7. Mid-career employees form a large portion of the workforce

Employees in the mid-career stage represent a significant segment of the organization and should be supported with career development and advancement opportunities.

8. Employee experience varies across the organization

The workforce includes employees with varying levels of experience, highlighting the importance of succession planning, mentoring, and targeted training.

Employees who simultaneously have:

Overtime = Yes
Low Job Satisfaction
